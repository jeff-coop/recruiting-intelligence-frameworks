# Technical Resume Signals

This page documents how I evaluate technical resumes and the signals I find most useful when assessing engineering talent.

The goal is not to create a checklist.

Strong candidates come from many different backgrounds.

Instead, I try to identify signals that provide insight into technical depth, ownership, learning ability, and long-term potential.

---

# First Principles

A resume is a summary.

It rarely tells the complete story.

The goal is not to judge candidates solely based on a resume.

The goal is to identify signals worth exploring further.

---

# Signal 1: Ownership

One of the strongest signals I look for.

Examples:

* Built systems from scratch
* Led migrations
* Owned critical services
* Launched products
* Improved reliability

Questions:

* What did they own?
* What decisions did they make?
* What was their impact?

---

# Signal 2: Technical Depth

Strong resumes often demonstrate depth rather than a collection of technologies.

Examples:

* Distributed Systems
* Infrastructure
* Databases
* AI Infrastructure
* Security
* Platform Engineering

Questions:

* How deeply did they work in a domain?
* What technical challenges did they solve?

---

# Signal 3: Scale

Scale provides useful context.

Examples:

* Millions of users
* Large datasets
* High-throughput systems
* Global infrastructure

Scale alone is not enough.

The candidate's role in achieving that scale matters more.

---

# Signal 4: Progression

Growth over time is often more interesting than titles.

Examples:

* Increased responsibility
* Larger projects
* Broader ownership
* Leadership opportunities

Questions:

* How has their career evolved?
* What patterns appear over time?

---

# Signal 5: Impact

Strong resumes explain outcomes.

Examples:

* Reduced latency
* Improved reliability
* Increased revenue
* Reduced infrastructure costs
* Improved developer productivity

Impact provides context beyond responsibilities.

---

# Signal 6: Curiosity

Examples:

* Open Source Contributions
* Personal Projects
* Technical Writing
* Research
* Teaching

These experiences often indicate learning outside of work.

---

# Signal 7: Leadership

Leadership appears in many forms.

Examples:

* Mentorship
* Technical Leadership
* Community Leadership
* Team Leadership

Titles alone do not determine leadership.

---

# Resume Sections I Pay Attention To

## Experience

Usually the most important section.

Questions:

* What problems did they solve?
* What impact did they create?
* What level of ownership did they have?

---

## Projects

Projects often reveal:

* Curiosity
* Learning
* Initiative

Particularly useful for:

* Students
* Early Career Candidates
* Career Changers

---

## Open Source

Examples:

* Maintainer Experience
* Contributions
* Documentation
* Community Involvement

---

## Research

Examples:

* Publications
* Research Labs
* Academic Projects
* Fellowships

---

# Signals I Care Less About

Examples:

* Long technology lists
* Buzzwords
* Generic responsibilities
* Years of experience alone

Without context, these signals are often weak.

---

# Questions I'm Trying To Answer

When reviewing a resume, I am usually asking:

* What motivates this person?
* What problems have they solved?
* How do they learn?
* What impact have they had?
* What signals suggest future growth?

---

# Final Observation

The best resumes often tell a story.

Not a story about technologies.

A story about growth, ownership, learning, and impact.
