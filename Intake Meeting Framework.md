# Intake Meeting Framework

This page documents how I approach intake meetings when launching a search.

The goal of an intake meeting is not to review the job description.

The goal is to understand the problem the business is trying to solve and align on what success looks like before engaging candidates.

Strong intake meetings often determine the success of the entire search.

---

# First Principle

Job descriptions rarely tell the full story.

Before sourcing begins, I want to understand:

* Why does this role exist?
* Why now?
* What business problem does this person solve?
* What does success look like?

The answers often shape the entire search strategy.

---

# Understanding The Business Context

Questions:

* Is this a growth hire or backfill?
* Why was headcount approved?
* What are the team's goals this year?
* What challenges exist today?

Understanding the business context helps prioritize the right signals.

---

# Defining Success

Questions:

* What should this person accomplish in 6 months?
* What should they accomplish in 12 months?
* What separates a good hire from a great hire?

The goal is to focus on outcomes, not keywords.

---

# Understanding The Team

Questions:

* Who will this person work with?
* How is the team structured?
* What skills already exist on the team?
* What gaps are we trying to fill?

Strong hires often complement existing teams.

---

# Technical Requirements

Questions:

* What skills are required on day one?
* What can be learned on the job?
* What technologies are most important?
* Which requirements are flexible?

The goal is to separate must-haves from nice-to-haves.

---

# Candidate Backgrounds

Questions:

* Where have successful hires come from?
* Which companies should we target?
* Which adjacent backgrounds could work?
* Which backgrounds typically do not work?

This helps shape market mapping efforts.

---

# Come Prepared With Data

One of the recruiter's responsibilities is bringing market intelligence into the conversation.

Hiring managers understand the role.

Recruiters should understand the market.

Examples:

* Talent availability
* Compensation benchmarks
* Geographic talent concentrations
* Competitor companies
* Candidate response rates
* Time-to-fill expectations

The goal is to help hiring managers make informed decisions before the search begins.

---

# Bring Example Profiles

Whenever possible, I like to bring example candidate profiles to the intake meeting.

Examples:

* Strong profiles
* Borderline profiles
* Adjacent backgrounds
* Transferable backgrounds

Questions:

* Would you interview this person?
* Why or why not?
* What stands out?
* What concerns you?

This often uncovers requirements that never appear in the job description.

---

# Identify Internal Talent Patterns

One of the most valuable exercises is understanding who is already successful inside the organization.

Questions:

* Who are the strongest performers on the team?
* What backgrounds do they have?
* What companies did they come from?
* What skills do they share?

Patterns often emerge quickly.

Those patterns can significantly improve targeting efforts.

---

# Challenge Assumptions

Recruiters should not simply take requirements at face value.

Part of the job is asking questions.

Examples:

* Does this skill truly need to be a requirement?
* Can this be learned?
* Are we limiting the talent pool unnecessarily?
* Is compensation aligned with expectations?

Constructive pushback often leads to better hiring outcomes.

---

# Market Reality Checks

Sometimes hiring managers want a candidate who does not realistically exist within budget, location, or timeline constraints.

Examples:

* Seniority expectations
* Compensation expectations
* Location restrictions
* Technical requirements

A recruiter's role is to provide market feedback early rather than allowing a search to fail slowly.

---

# Compensation & Logistics

Questions:

* What is the compensation range?
* How flexible is the budget?
* How competitive is the market?
* Are relocation or remote options available?

Recruiting strategy should reflect market realities.

---

# Interview Process

Questions:

* Who is involved?
* How many stages exist?
* What is each stage evaluating?
* What is the target timeline?

Candidates value clarity and speed.

---

# Calibration

The first few candidate conversations are often the most important.

Questions:

* Are we seeing the right profiles?
* Are expectations realistic?
* What patterns are emerging?

Early calibration prevents wasted effort.

---

# Common Intake Mistakes

Examples:

* Treating the job description as the full requirement
* Too many must-haves
* No agreement on success criteria
* No understanding of market conditions
* Lack of interview consistency

Most recruiting problems can be traced back to weak alignment at intake.

---

# Questions I'm Trying To Answer

When leaving an intake meeting, I want to know:

* What problem are we solving?
* What does success look like?
* What backgrounds are most relevant?
* What tradeoffs are acceptable?
* How will candidates be evaluated?

---

# Final Observation

The best intake meetings create clarity.

When recruiters and hiring managers have a shared understanding of success, the rest of the search becomes significantly easier.
