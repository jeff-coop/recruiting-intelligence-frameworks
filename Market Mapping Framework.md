# Market Mapping Framework

This page documents how I approach market mapping for technical recruiting and talent intelligence.

The goal is not to build a list of names.

The goal is to understand a talent market, identify where strong candidates are likely to be found, and develop a structured view of the ecosystem.

Market mapping helps answer one simple question:

Where does the talent live?

---

# What Is Market Mapping?

Market mapping is the process of identifying:

* Companies
* Teams
* Talent Pools
* Communities
* Universities
* Research Labs
* Open Source Ecosystems

that are relevant to a hiring objective.

Before reaching out to candidates, I want to understand the market.

---

# Step 1: Define The Problem

Before building a map, I try to understand:

* Why does this role exist?
* What problem does this person solve?
* What skills are truly required?
* What backgrounds are likely to succeed?

The goal is to avoid mapping the wrong market.

---

# Step 2: Identify Target Companies

The first layer is usually companies.

Questions:

* Which companies hire similar talent?
* Which companies solve similar problems?
* Which companies operate at similar scale?

Examples:

Infrastructure Search:

* Databricks
* Snowflake
* MongoDB
* Confluent
* Datadog

AI Search:

* OpenAI
* Anthropic
* Cohere
* Scale AI

---

# Step 3: Identify Adjacent Talent Pools

Strong candidates do not always come from obvious competitors.

Questions:

* What backgrounds transfer well?
* What adjacent domains exist?

Examples:

Infrastructure Candidates May Come From:

* Platform Engineering
* Developer Productivity
* Site Reliability Engineering
* Cloud Engineering

---

# Step 4: Identify Talent Communities

Many strong candidates spend time in technical communities.

Examples:

* Open Source Projects
* Conferences
* Meetups
* Research Communities
* Competitive Programming

These communities often reveal emerging talent before recruiters find them.

---

# Step 5: Identify University Pipelines

For early talent searches:

Questions:

* Which universities produce relevant talent?
* Which labs are strongest?
* Which clubs are most active?

Examples:

* MIT
* Stanford
* Carnegie Mellon
* Berkeley
* Georgia Tech

---

# Step 6: Identify Signal Sources

Beyond resumes.

Examples:

* GitHub
* Research Publications
* Conferences
* Open Source Projects
* Fellowships
* Competitions

Signals often provide better insight than titles.

---

# Building A Market Map

A typical market map may include:

## Companies

Where talent currently works.

## Universities

Where talent develops.

## Communities

Where talent learns.

## Open Source

Where talent contributes.

## Conferences

Where talent gathers.

---

# Questions I Try To Answer

When building a market map, I am usually asking:

* Where is the highest concentration of talent?
* Which companies produce strong candidates?
* Which adjacent pools exist?
* Which signals matter most?
* Where can future talent be identified?

---

# Common Mistakes

Examples:

* Focusing only on direct competitors
* Ignoring adjacent talent pools
* Overvaluing company logos
* Ignoring technical communities
* Ignoring early talent pipelines

The best candidates often come from unexpected places.

---

# Final Observation

Market mapping is not about finding candidates.

Market mapping is about understanding ecosystems.

The better I understand the ecosystem, the more likely I am to find exceptional talent.
