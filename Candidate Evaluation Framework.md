# Candidate Evaluation Framework

This framework represents how I think about evaluating technical talent.

The goal is not to create a scorecard or ranking system.

Exceptional talent comes from many different backgrounds and experiences.

Instead, this framework helps organize observations and identify signals that may indicate long-term potential, technical depth, and impact.

---

# Principle 1: Focus On Signals, Not Keywords

Keywords can help identify candidates.

Signals help understand candidates.

Examples:

Weak Signals:

* Technology Keywords
* Company Logos
* School Names

Stronger Signals:

* Ownership
* Technical Depth
* Curiosity
* Learning Velocity
* Leadership
* Communication

---

# Principle 2: Context Matters

The same accomplishment can mean very different things depending on context.

Questions:

* What challenges did the candidate face?
* What resources were available?
* What constraints existed?
* How large was the problem?

Impact should always be evaluated within context.

---

# Pillar 1: Technical Depth

Questions:

* What problems have they solved?
* How complex were those problems?
* How deeply do they understand their domain?

Examples:

* Distributed Systems
* Infrastructure
* AI
* Security
* Databases
* Platform Engineering

---

# Pillar 2: Ownership

One of the strongest indicators of future growth.

Examples:

* Built systems from scratch
* Led initiatives
* Solved ambiguous problems
* Improved reliability
* Drove adoption

Questions:

* Did they create leverage?
* Did they take responsibility?

---

# Pillar 3: Learning Velocity

Technology evolves quickly.

The ability to learn often matters more than current knowledge.

Signals:

* Open Source
* Side Projects
* Research
* Technical Writing
* Continuous Learning

---

# Pillar 4: Curiosity

Strong candidates often demonstrate curiosity outside of their day-to-day responsibilities.

Examples:

* Personal Projects
* Technical Blogs
* Open Source
* Community Involvement

---

# Pillar 5: Communication

Examples:

* Documentation
* Technical Writing
* Mentorship
* Presentations

Questions:

* Can they explain complexity clearly?
* Can they influence others?

---

# Pillar 6: Leadership

Leadership does not require management experience.

Examples:

* Mentorship
* Technical Leadership
* Community Leadership
* Team Leadership

---

# What I Care Less About

Examples:

* Number of Keywords
* Number of Certifications
* Years of Experience Alone
* Prestige Without Impact

---

# Questions I Try To Answer

* What motivates this person?
* How do they learn?
* What impact have they had?
* What environments helped them grow?
* What signals suggest future growth?

---

# Final Observation

No framework predicts success perfectly.

The goal is not to find perfect candidates.

The goal is to better understand people, identify potential, and make more informed decisions.
