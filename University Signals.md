# University Signals

This page documents how I think about universities when evaluating technical talent.

The goal is not to rank schools.

Exceptional engineers come from every type of university.

Instead, I try to understand the experiences, environments, and opportunities that may help students develop technical depth, curiosity, and long-term potential.

University name is one signal among many.

Context matters far more.

---

# First Principle

A university is an environment.

Not an outcome.

What a student does within that environment is often more important than the institution itself.

Examples:

* Research
* Open Source
* Internships
* Leadership
* Projects
* Competitions

---

# Signals I Find Most Interesting

## Research

Examples:

* Research Assistant
* Publications
* Research Labs
* Independent Research

Research often demonstrates:

* Curiosity
* Persistence
* Problem Solving

---

## Technical Projects

Examples:

* Open Source Contributions
* Hackathon Projects

Projects often reveal initiative and learning outside the classroom.

# University Tiers

I generally think about universities in terms of ecosystems rather than rankings.

Examples:

## Research Powerhouses

* MIT
* Stanford
* Carnegie Mellon
* Berkeley
* Princeton

## Strong Engineering Programs

* Georgia Tech
* UIUC
* Michigan
* Purdue
* UT Austin

## Emerging Talent Sources

Strong talent frequently comes from:

* State Schools
* Regional Universities
* Community College Transfer Programs
* Military Backgrounds

---

# Signals I Care Less About

Examples:

* School Name Alone
* GPA Alone
* Prestige Alone

Without context, these signals are often weak.

---

# Questions I'm Trying To Answer

When reviewing a university background, I am usually asking:

* What opportunities did this person pursue?
* What did they build?
* What did they learn?
* What communities did they participate in?
* What signals suggest future growth?

---

# Final Observation

Universities create opportunities.

Students create outcomes.

The strongest signals usually come from what people choose to do with the opportunities available to them.
