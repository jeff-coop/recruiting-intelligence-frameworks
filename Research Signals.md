# Research Signals

This page documents how I think about research experience when evaluating technical talent.

The goal is not to prioritize academic backgrounds over industry experience.

The goal is to understand what research experience may reveal about a person's interests, problem-solving abilities, curiosity, and learning habits.

Research is one signal among many.

---

# First Principle

Research experience is often a signal of curiosity.

Researchers spend significant time exploring questions that may not have obvious answers.

Examples:

* Artificial Intelligence
* Machine Learning
* Distributed Systems
* Databases
* Security
* Robotics
* Networking

Research often develops deep problem-solving skills.

---

# What Research Can Signal

Examples:

* Curiosity
* Persistence
* Technical Depth
* Analytical Thinking
* Learning Velocity

Research rarely follows a clear path.

Many projects require experimentation, iteration, and failure before success.

---

# Publications

Publications can provide useful context.

Questions:

* What problems were explored?
* What was the candidate's contribution?
* How technical was the work?

Publication count alone is not particularly meaningful.

The work behind the publication matters more.

---

# Research Labs

Research labs often provide strong learning environments.

Examples:

* Artificial Intelligence Labs
* Systems Labs
* Security Labs
* Robotics Labs
* Human Computer Interaction Labs

Lab experience frequently exposes students to complex technical challenges.

---

# Independent Research

One of the most interesting signals.

Examples:

* Personal Research Projects
* Independent Studies
* Open Research Contributions

Independent research often demonstrates initiative.

---

# Research + Engineering

A combination I find particularly interesting.

Examples:

* Research Engineers
* Applied Scientists
* Machine Learning Engineers
* Infrastructure Researchers

These individuals often bridge theory and practical implementation.

---

# AI Research Signals

Examples:

* Publications
* Research Assistantships
* Open Source Contributions
* Model Development
* Benchmarking

Questions:

* Can they move from theory to implementation?
* Can they explain their work clearly?

---

# Infrastructure Research Signals

Examples:

* Distributed Systems Research
* Database Research
* Networking Research
* Operating Systems Research

These areas often require strong technical foundations.

---

# Communication Signals

Research frequently involves:

* Writing
* Presenting
* Defending Ideas
* Explaining Complex Concepts

Strong communication often emerges alongside strong research experience.

---

# Signals I Find Most Interesting

Examples:

* Long-Term Research Projects
* Independent Research
* Research + Engineering Experience
* Publications With Clear Contributions
* Open Source Research Projects

---

# Signals I Care Less About

Examples:

* Publication Count Alone
* University Prestige Alone
* Research Titles Alone

Context always matters.

---

# Questions I'm Trying To Answer

When reviewing research experience, I am usually asking:

* What motivated this work?
* What problems were explored?
* How difficult were the challenges?
* What was the candidate's contribution?
* What does this experience reveal about how they learn?

---

# Final Observation

Research is often less about finding answers and more about learning how to ask better questions.

That mindset frequently appears among exceptional engineers, scientists, and technical leaders.
